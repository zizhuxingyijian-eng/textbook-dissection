Weekly Drill-Down
Phase 1–2 · Post-War Reconstruction & High Growth
W2Post-War Reforms (1945–1949)
▼

GHQ dismantled wartime structures and fought hyperinflation before handing sovereignty back to Japan.

Three GHQ Reforms

Zaibatsu Dissolution

Broke up family-owned conglomerates to reduce monopoly power and democratize economic control.

Land Reform

Redistributed farm land from absentee landlords to tenant farmers → equalized rural income.

Labor Law Reform

Guaranteed workers right to unionize. Seeds of Japan's later lifetime-employment system.

Hyperinflation Mechanism

Wartime: BoJ printed money (RFC bonds)→ Limited supply of goods→ Hyperinflation→ Emergency deposit freeze (1946)→ Inflation continues

Dodge Line (1949)

  • Core idea: balanced budget — no new government bonds, stop RFC lending
  • Cut subsidies on industrial goods → inflation rate fell but caused recession
  • Toyota nearly collapsed. Saved by Korean War procurement (1950–52)

Shoup Report

  • Built progressive income tax system — higher rates for richer people
  • Goal: empower ordinary people, democratize Japan's income distribution
W2–3High Growth Era (1950–1973)
▼

Japan's "economic miracle" — avg real GDP growth ~10%/yr — powered by balanced growth in A, K, and H.

Y = A × F(K, H)    [GDP = Technology × f(Capital, Labor Efficiency)]

↑ H (Labor)

Rural→urban migration, higher education, lifetime employment → skill accumulation via on-the-job training.

↑ K (Capital)

High household savings funneled through banks to industrial investment. Convoy system ensured stability.

↑ A (Technology)

Technology catch-up from the West, absorption of advanced production methods in manufacturing.

Financial System (Convoy System)

  • Financial repression: government capped deposit rates → low lending rates → cheap capital for firms
  • Banks segmented: long-term credit banks (large firms), city banks (working capital), regional banks (SMEs)
  • FILP: postal savings + pension funds → Trust Fund Bureau → Development Bank of Japan → strategic industries
  • Cross-shareholding (Keiretsu): 6 major groups (Mitsubishi, Mitsui, Sumitomo…) — banks own shares in clients for stability

International Capital Controls

  • Bretton Woods: 1$ = 360¥ (fixed rate). Japan needed capital controls to defend the peg.
  • Foreign Exchange Act (1949): firms must deposit foreign currency earnings at government accounts
  • World Bank loans financed bullet trains, highways, dams — circumvented capital controls
Phase 3 · External Shocks & Adjustment
W3Bretton Woods Collapse & Oil Shocks (1971–1979)
▼

The US fiscal-external imbalance destroyed the gold peg. Japan then absorbed two oil shocks in very different ways — the contrast reveals how macroeconomic learning works.

Why Bretton Woods Collapsed — Twin Deficit Mechanism

National Savings Identity: (Y − T − C) − I = CA surplus = (X − M) + Net Transfers
  • Vietnam War + Great Society social programs → US fiscal deficit (G > T)
  • Fiscal deficit must be financed: either crowds out private investment or draws in foreign savings (= CA deficit)
  • CA deficit → dollars piling up in foreign central banks → too many dollars relative to US gold reserves
  • Confidence in $35/oz gold peg erodes → speculative outflows → the peg becomes unsustainable

Nixon Shock (August 15, 1971)

Nixon announces: USD–gold convertibility suspended→ Smithsonian Agreement Dec 1971: dollar devalued, bands widened (failed within 15 months)→ Feb–Mar 1973: major currencies move to free float→ JPY: 360 → ~270 ¥/$ by late 1973
For Japan, floating immediately became painful: export competitiveness fell. The government's response — BOJ rate cuts + fiscal expansion — was the IS-LM textbook playbook, but mistimed.

1st Oil Shock (October 1973) — How Japan Was Hit

  • OPEC oil embargo raised oil price from ~$3 → ~$12/barrel (×4 in 3 months)
  • Japan imports ~99% of its oil → direct cost pass-through: input prices surge
  • AS-AD effect: negative supply shock → AD curve shifts left (lower output) AND AS curve shifts left (higher prices) → stagflation
  • 1974 CPI inflation: ~23% — highest in post-war Japan

BOJ Response (1974)

Aggressive rate hikes — discount rate raised to 9%. Broke inflationary expectations. Inflation fell to ~3% by 1975.

Fiscal Expansion

Gov't issued bonds (↑G) to cushion demand. Origin of Japan's government debt accumulation — 1973 was the turning point.

FX Recovery

Tight monetary policy attracted capital inflows → yen stabilized. Current account improved as import demand fell and efficiency gains took hold.

Debt origin: Before 1973, Japan ran near-balanced budgets. The 1973 shock forced bond issuance that was never fully reversed — laying the foundation for later debt problems.

2nd Oil Shock (1979) — Why Japan Did Better

  • Iran Revolution → oil price doubles again (~$13 → $35/barrel)
  • Japan's response was qualitatively different from 1973:
  • Pre-emptive BOJ tightening before inflation expectations could become entrenched (learned from 1974)
  • Energy efficiency investments from mid-1970s: Japan had already restructured away from oil-intensive heavy industry
  • CPI peak in 1980: ~8% vs. 23% in 1974 — controlled at roughly 1/3 of 1st shock impact
  • "Japan as No. 1" (1979): US academic Ezra Vogel's book celebrated Japan's management style and growth model — but this overconfidence planted seeds of complacency going into the bubble years
Phase 4 · Financial Liberalization & Bubble Formation
W4Financial Liberalization & Bubble Formation (1980–1990)
▼

Floating exchange rates made capital controls self-defeating. Deregulation stripped banks of their core corporate clients, forcing a dangerous pivot to real estate — the fuel for the bubble.

Financial Disintermediation — The Core Structural Shock

1980: Capital flow liberalization (Foreign Exchange Act revised)→ 1984: Euro-yen market opened; FX futures deregulated→ Domestic CP + bond markets deregulated→ Toyota, Sony, Panasonic issue bonds directly at lower cost→ Bank loan demand from large firms collapses
  • City banks and long-term credit banks had been built to serve these firms — they had no alternative revenue
  • Pivoted to real estate developers, construction companies, non-bank lenders (jūsen)
  • These clients had no bond-market alternative → banks had pricing power → higher margins
  • But banks were inexperienced at assessing these borrowers → substituted land appraisals for credit analysis
  • Fatal assumption: "land prices in Japan never fall" — based on post-war experience only

External Pressure: Volcker Shock → Plaza Accord

  • Volcker Shock (1979–82): Fed Funds Rate raised to ~20% to break US inflation → global capital flows to USD → dollar surged ~50% vs major currencies
  • Strong USD hurt US exporters and widened US CA deficit — political pressure built for G5 coordination
  • Plaza Accord (September 22, 1985): G5 finance ministers agreed to jointly intervene in FX markets to depreciate the dollar
  • UIP (Uncovered Interest Parity) channel: market anticipated future yen appreciation → yen rose even faster than intervention alone could explain
  • Result: JPY moved from ~240 ¥/$ (1985) to ~120 ¥/$ by 1988 — a 50% appreciation in 3 years

UIP: Why Markets Moved Faster Than Policy

UIP: i_JP = i_US + E[Δe]    → if yen expected to rise, JP rates can be lower
  • Once Plaza Accord was announced, agents expected yen to appreciate → they bought yen now → yen appreciated immediately
  • Rational expectations caused overshoot: by 1987–88 yen was arguably over-appreciated
  • Japan's manufacturing recession (endaka fukyo — "high-yen recession") of 1986 followed

The BOJ's Paradox (1987–1989)

  • BOJ was under dual pressure: counter export slowdown (cut rates) AND maintain G7 coordination (keep rates low after Black Monday, Oct 1987)
  • Consumer price inflation remained low — oil prices had fallen and the yen appreciation was making imports cheap
  • No CPI signal to tighten — but asset prices (land, equities) were rising 20–30%/yr
  • BOJ kept discount rate at 2.5% from Feb 1987 to May 1989 — longest low-rate period in post-war history

Asset Pricing Bubble — The Formal Model

P_t = E[d_{t+1} + P_{t+1}] / (1 + r_t)

Channel 1: r↓

Lower discount rate raises the present value of all future dividends and prices. With r at historic lows, even modest expected returns justify very high current prices.

Channel 2: E[P↑]

If everyone expects prices to keep rising, rational agents bid up today's price. Self-fulfilling — until the expectation reverses.

Bubble Condition

When E[P_{t+1}] grows faster than (1+r), price diverges from fundamentals (d). Bubble = the portion of P not backed by d.

By 1989 the Nikkei implied a P/E ratio of ~60×. Tokyo land prices implied it would cost more than the entire US real estate market to buy all of metropolitan Tokyo. Both were pure bubble components in the pricing formula.
Phase 5 · Bubble Collapse & Crisis Build-Up (1990–1997)
W5aBubble Collapse (1989–1991)
▼

Three simultaneous policy levers punctured the bubble. The asset pricing formula worked in reverse — and the collateral underneath Japan's entire banking system collapsed.

Three Policy Levers (1989–90)

BOJ Rate Hikes

Discount rate raised from 2.5% (May 1989) to 6% (Aug 1990) in 5 steps. Direct hit on P = E[d+P']/(1+r): as r↑, P must fall.

MoF Land Loan Quota

March 1990: Ministry of Finance ordered banks to cap growth in real estate loans below total loan growth. Killed the supply of credit to the sector.

Land Value Tax (1991)

Capital gains tax on land appreciation introduced. Reduced incentive to hold land speculatively → sellers entered market.

Asset Price Formula in Reverse

P_t = E[d + P_{t+1}] / (1 + r_t)  →  r↑ + E[P_{t+1}]↓ → P_t collapses non-linearly
  • Nikkei 225: 38,915 (Dec 1989) → 15,000 (Aug 1992) — −61%
  • Tokyo commercial land: peak 1991 → fell ~70% by 2000
  • Both channels hit simultaneously: r rose AND expectations reversed → double amplification

W5bJūsen Crisis (1995)
▼

Jūsen (住専) were non-bank mortgage lenders set up by city banks in the 1970s. They became the worst NPL black hole — and their bailout turned into a political scandal about who should bear the losses.

Structure & Loophole

  • Jūsen were created to handle retail housing loans (banks found them too small/costly to service directly)
  • In the late 1980s, after the MoF land loan quota was applied to banks, jūsen were exempt — they kept lending aggressively into real estate at the peak
  • Funded by borrowing from parent banks (city banks), agricultural cooperatives (JA), and the Norinchukin Bank
  • When land collapsed: jūsen held ¥13T in loans; ¥8.4T (64%) were classified as non-performing by 1995

W5c1996 Financial Reforms — Partial Fix
▼

The jūsen crisis forced Japan to modernize its deposit insurance and resolution framework. The new tools were real — but a critical gap was left unfilled.

Deposit Insurance Corporation of Japan (DICJ) — New Tools

Purchase & Assumption

DICJ could arrange takeover of a failing bank by a healthy acquirer — pays acquirer to assume deposits and selected assets.

Bridge Bank

Temporary government-run bank that takes over a failed institution's operations while a buyer is found. Prevents sudden credit disruption.

NPL Purchases

Resolution and Collection Corporation (RCC) could buy bad loans at discount from failed institutions, managing workout over time.

The Critical Gap

No mechanism for pre-failure recapitalization. DICJ tools only triggered after a bank failed — by which point panic had already spread. There was no authority to inject capital into a weakened-but-not-yet-failed bank to prevent the failure from happening. This gap was fatally exposed in 1997.
  • "Big Bang" financial liberalization also announced in 1996: brokerage commissions deregulated, securities and banking activities to be separated, foreign competition encouraged
  • Well-designed in principle — but the timing (before the NPL crisis was resolved) accelerated the 1997 crisis
W5d1997 Double Misjudgment
▼

1997 was the year two decisions combined to push Japan over the edge: premature fiscal tightening just as the banking system was breaking down.

Consumption Tax Hike (April 1997): 3% → 5%

  • Motivated by fiscal consolidation goals set at the G7 and MOF pressure to reduce bond issuance
  • Timing: economy had shown signs of recovery in 1996 — MOF believed the cycle had turned
  • Effect: sharp Q1 1997 consumption boom (front-loading before hike) followed by severe Q2–Q3 contraction
  • Private consumption fell 3.5% in real terms in fiscal 1997 — directly amplifying the banking crisis

Big Bang Liberalization — Right Reform, Wrong Timing

  • Deregulation increased competition and transparency — which exposed how weak the banks actually were
  • International investors and counterparties began demanding higher rates from Japanese banks (Japan Premium)
  • Liberalization without prior resolution of the NPL stock was like performing surgery without anesthesia
The combination — fiscal drag of ~2% of GDP from tax hike + banking confidence collapse — produced Japan's worst post-war recession to that point. Policymakers had mistimed both fiscal policy and structural reform sequencing.
W5e1997–98 Banking Crisis & Resolution
▼

A cascade of bank failures in late 1997 froze the interbank market and produced the Japan Premium — a measurable signal of systemic distrust.

Cascade of Failures

Sanyo Securities (Nov 1997): first post-war default on call loan market→ Call loan market freezes: banks stop lending overnight to each other→ Hokkaido Takushoku Bank fails (Nov 17)→ Yamaichi Securities (Nov 24): largest securities firm; self-liquidation→ Long-Term Credit Bank (Oct 1998) + Nippon Credit Bank (Dec 1998): nationalized

The Stigma Problem

  • BOJ offered emergency liquidity — but banks were afraid to borrow from it ("stigma": being seen at the BOJ window signaled distress)
  • Banks hoarded reserves instead of lending → credit crunch: even healthy firms couldn't get loans
  • GDP fell ~2% in fiscal 1998; unemployment rate hit post-war high of 4.4%

Japan Premium (TIBOR − LIBOR)

Japan Premium = TIBOR_JP − LIBOR_USD    (peaked ~1% in late 1997)
  • Foreign banks refused to lend to Japanese banks at normal interbank rates — demanded a premium for counterparty risk
  • Measurable in the spread between Tokyo Interbank Offered Rate and London equivalent
  • Signaled that international markets had lost confidence in the solvency of Japan's banking system

Resolution: ¥7.45T Equity Injection (1998–99)

  • Financial Revitalization Law (Oct 1998) and Financial Function Early Strengthening Law created framework for public recapitalization
  • 21 major banks received a total of ¥7.45 trillion in public equity capital injections
  • LTCB and NCB nationalized, NPLs carved out to RCC, eventual sale to foreign investors (Ripplewood Holdings bought LTCB)

Japan vs. Sweden: Crisis Resolution Comparison

Japan (1990–1999)
  • Delayed recognition of NPLs for ~7 years
  • No pre-failure recapitalization tools until 1998
  • Political interference (JA, LDP) slowed resolution
  • Convoy system protected weak banks too long
  • Result: "Lost Decade" — stagnation through 2000s
Sweden (1991–1994)
  • Immediate recognition and public disclosure of bad loans
  • State took equity stakes (nationalization) quickly
  • Bad assets carved into government "bad banks"
  • Transparent loss allocation — no political favoritism
  • Result: recovery within 3 years; model for IMF playbook
Core lesson: Speed and transparency of loss recognition determine the length of a financial crisis. Japan's delayed, opaque approach multiplied the eventual cost; Sweden's rapid, transparent approach compressed it.
Week 6 · Banking Crisis — Detailed Notes
W6a1997 Banking Crisis: Mechanisms & Contagion
▼

The Sanyo Securities collapse in November 1997 mattered not because of its size, but because of the precedent: the government would no longer bail out failing institutions. That signal froze the call loan market and triggered a cascade of failures.

The Trigger: Sanyo Securities (Nov 3, 1997)

  • What happened: Sanyo Securities (mid-sized securities firm) filed for bankruptcy. Its non-bank subsidiary had bad real-estate loans from the 1980s bubble
  • Previously, MoF used moral suasion to force healthy institutions to absorb failing ones — the convoy system handled crises quietly
  • This time: no law existed for government to buy securities firm shares; BoJ could not lend to an insolvent institution
  • Sanyo defaulted on call loans — the first major call-loan default in modern Japan
  • Market interpreted this as: "the government will no longer bail out failing institutions" → convoy system ended here
Call loan (同業拆借): short-term borrowing of central bank reserves between financial institutions to settle daily payments. Normally considered risk-free — Sanyo's default shattered this and made every bank mistrust every other bank overnight.

Contagion: Hokkaido Takushoku & Yamaichi (Nov 1997)

InstitutionTypeProblem & Resolution
Hokkaido Takushoku BankCity bankBad commercial real-estate loans + failed merger → deposit withdrawals. DICJ gave ¥1.7T subsidy + bought ¥1.6T of NPLs. Business sold to Hokuyo Bank & Chuo Trust (Nov 17)
Yamaichi SecuritiesOne of top 4 securities firmsHidden losses from illegally guaranteeing client trading losses; Fuji Bank refused to lend. Voluntary liquidation (Nov 22). BoJ lent funds for orderly repayment. Formally bankrupt 1999, liquidation completed 2005

First Wave of Policy Responses (Dec 1997 – Feb 1998)

Dec 5, 1997: MoF guarantees all bank deposits & debentures until March 2001→ Feb 1998: Diet passes acts giving DICJ ¥10T of JGBs + ¥20T government guarantee→ DICJ can buy equity in surviving banks (up to ¥13T) and guarantee failed-bank deposits (up to ¥17T)→ Stigma problem: banks each take same small token injection — only ¥1.8T total absorbed
Stigma problem: If one bank accepted a large injection, markets would assume it was the weakest → confidence crash. So every bank took the same token amount. Result: banks remained undercapitalized and vulnerable.

LTCB & NCB Collapse — Stronger Legislation (Late 1998)

  • Summer 1998: Stock prices of Long-Term Credit Bank (LTCB) and Nippon Credit Bank (NCB) crashed — long-term credit banks were most damaged by 1980s financial disintermediation
  • Oct 1998: Diet passed stronger acts: created RCC (Resolution and Collection Corporation) — DICJ subsidiary that absorbs and collects NPLs
  • DICJ could now take over troubled banks before bankruptcy, transferring NPLs to RCC — the key gap from 1996 was finally filled
  • LTCB nationalized Oct 1998 → sold to investment funds in 2000, renamed Shinsei Bank
  • NCB nationalized Dec 1998 → renamed Aozora Bank
  • March 1999: DICJ injected ¥7.45 trillion of equity into 15 large banks → no more large failures after this

Main Consequence: Credit Crunch

  • Even with deposit insurance protecting depositors, banks needed interbank lending to function daily
  • Banks doubted each other's asset quality → interbank lending froze → all banks tightened lending standards to clean up balance sheets
  • New bank loans for fixed investment fell by ~¥10 trillion; real GDP growth turned negative
  • Bank lending rates did NOT rise — counterintuitive but logical: banks weren't trying to extract higher returns, they were trying to survive by avoiding risky loans entirely
  • Small/medium-sized firms hit hardest as riskier borrowers — their share of bank loans dropped sharply

Japan Premium (TIBOR − LIBOR)

Japan Premium = TIBOR (USD loans to Japanese banks, Tokyo) − LIBOR (USD loans to global banks, London) ≈ 0.3% at peak
  • Foreign banks charged extra interest on dollar loans to Japanese banks — suspected hidden losses in their portfolios
  • Originated after 1995 Daiwa Bank scandal: NY branch hid trading losses from the Fed, alerting global markets to opacity in Japanese banks
  • Two visible spikes: post-Sanyo collapse (late 1997) and LTCB/NCB period (summer 1998 – early 1999)

Aftermath & Resolution (2000s)

  • 2000: LTCB and NCB sold back to private investors (Shinsei, Aozora)
  • 2001: Government launched the Financial Revitalization Program — goal: clear NPLs by 2004; RCC actively purchased NPLs and collected on them
  • 2005: Unlimited deposit guarantee ended; reverted to standard ¥10 million per depositor
  • NPLs returned to normal background levels by early 2000s; land prices kept falling throughout — but banks' balance sheets were finally cleaned

W6bJapan vs. Sweden — Why Crisis Length Differed
▼

Same root cause — financial liberalization + real estate bubble — radically different crisis durations. Sweden resolved its banking crisis in ~3 years; Japan took over a decade. The key variable was not the severity of losses, but the speed of government response.

1. Shared Background

  • Financial liberalization occurred across industrialized countries in the 1980s, increasing competition among banks in both Japan and Sweden
  • Both countries expanded bank lending heavily into commercial real estate (loans to property developers, not home buyers) → large losses in the 1990s
  • Key point: Japan's crisis was not unique — it was part of a global pattern triggered by financial liberalization

2. Sweden's Bubble — Identical Mechanism

  • Real interest rates were lowered in the second half of the 1980s; Swedish banks expanded lending into commercial real estate after liberalization in 1983–85
  • Commercial real estate prices rose sharply, then collapsed in the early 1990s — but began recovering after 1993
  • Banks that expanded fastest suffered worst losses: Nordbanken lost 21.4% of total lending; Gota lost 37.3%

3. Sweden's Response — Fast & Decisive

Swedish banks recognized loan losses in the early 1990s — unlike Japanese banks, which delayed until the late 1990s.

Securum = Sweden's "bad bank" — a state company set up to buy non-performing loans from banks to stabilize them. Created 1992, dissolved 1997 after completing its mission. Japan created a similar agency (RCC) in 1998 — the same year Sweden finished cleaning up. A decade's difference.

4. Japan's Delayed Response — 4 Reasons

  • ① Jyu-sen politics (1995): the unfair distribution of losses in the Jyu-sen dissolution made it politically toxic for the government to bail out banks — the public saw it as protecting insiders at taxpayers' expense
  • ② LDP leadership change: the LDP lost power in the 1993 general election due to bribery scandals — weakening political will and capacity for bold action at the critical moment
  • ③ Legal vacuum: former senior bureaucrats confirmed there was no legal authority to recapitalize banks swiftly in the early 1990s — the legal framework simply didn't exist yet

6. The Normative Trade-off: Fairness vs. Speed

Bailouts work economically — they stop credit crunches and restore the banking system fast. Sweden proved this. But bailouts are politically toxic — the government absorbs bank losses while ordinary people who lost jobs get nothing. There is a genuine trade-off between fairness and quick resolution. Consequence: rise of anti-inequality sentiment → "top 1%" discourse → political conditions partly contributing to Trump's 2016 victory. The sense of fairness is not a trivial issue — which option is better is a genuine open question.

Four Causes of Rising Non-Regular Employment

(a) Industrial Shift to Services

Services use more non-regular labor by nature. As services' GDP share grew after yen appreciation (~1993), non-regular share rose mechanically.

(b) Banking Crisis (1997–98)

Regular workers' wages cannot be cut quickly → firms became cautious about hiring regulars. New entrants found it hard to land regular jobs.

(c) Worker Dispatching Act Revisions

1999: Dispatched work allowed in ANY profession. 2004: Extended to manufacturing. Increased the legal supply of non-regular labor.

(d) Aging (see W7b)

Older workers near retirement (60s) often switch to contractor / part-time status, mechanically raising the non-regular share.

How the Dispatch System Works

  • Staffing company sends worker to client company; client directs the worker and pays an hourly fee to the staffing company

Two Approaches

Model-Based (Structural)

Define how X relates to Y mathematically. Use regression, IV. "I have an idea — can the math prove it?"

Causal Inference

Ask where the variation in X comes from. Tools: RCT, matching, IV, DiD. Focused on identification.

ATE = E[Y(X=1) − Y(X=0)]
  • ITE (Individual Treatment Effect) = generally unknowable → aim for ATE
  • Causality ≠ correlation: correlation is symmetric; causality is directional
  • Data alone can't tell causality — need theory, design, or assumptions
  • Structural estimation: model-based counterfactuals ("what if we build an airport?") — valid only if model is correct
W3Potential Outcomes & Rubin Causal Model
▼
  • Potential outcomes: Y(1) = outcome if treated, Y(0) = outcome if untreated — only one is observable (fundamental problem)
  • Selection bias: naive difference E[Y|X=1] − E[Y|X=0] confounds treatment effect with pre-existing differences
  • Unconfoundedness (ignorability): Y(0),Y(1) ⊥ X | covariates Z → treatment as-good-as-random given Z
  • RCT achieves unconfoundedness by design (random assignment)
W4Endogeneity & Exogeneity
▼
  • Endogeneity: X is correlated with the error term ε → OLS estimate biased
  • Sources: omitted variable bias, reverse causality, measurement error
  • Exogenous variation: variation in X that is "as-good-as-random" for purposes of identifying the causal effect
Core insight: finding exogenous variation in X is the central challenge of all causal inference methods.

W2Comparative Advantage (Ricardian Model)
▼

The most fundamental result in trade theory: trade is driven by relative, not absolute, cost differences.

  • Law of Comparative Advantage: a country should specialize in goods where its opportunity cost is lower
  • One country CANNOT have comparative advantage in both goods (due to reciprocal opportunity costs)
  • Absolute advantage has NO importance for the pattern of trade (but matters for income distribution)
  • Autarky price: equilibrium relative price when trade is banned → found by RS = RD intersection
  • Trade opens: countries face the world relative price → specialize accordingly → both gain
  • "Self-sufficiency" (autarky) arguments like Nigeria's rice import ban are rejected by comparative advantage logic
Pattern of trade determined by comparative advantage, not absolute advantage
W3Relative Supply & Demand in Trade Equilibrium
▼
  • Relative Supply (RS): (Qwine/Qcloth) as a function of (Pwine/Pcloth) — upward sloping
  • Relative Demand (RD): world demand for wine relative to cloth — downward sloping
  • World equilibrium: intersection of RS and RD gives the terms of trade
  • Home exports good with lower autarky relative price, imports the other
Key: the trade equilibrium price lies between the two autarky prices. Both countries gain from trade relative to autarky.
W4Trade & Politics — Winners and Losers
▼
  • Stolper-Samuelson theorem: trade raises the real wage of the abundant factor, lowers that of the scarce factor
  • In rich countries (capital-abundant): trade ↑ returns to capital, ↓ wages of low-skilled labor → political tension
  • Protectionism is a political equilibrium: losing factors lobby for tariffs (e.g., steel workers, farmers)
  • GATT/WTO: multilateral system to credibly commit countries to low tariffs and prevent trade wars
  • Trump tariffs example: 96% of burden passed to US buyers, not foreign exporters
W5Returns to Scale & New Trade Theory
▼
  • Increasing returns to scale (IRS): average costs fall as output rises → tendency toward concentration
  • Intra-industry trade: similar countries trading similar goods (e.g., Germany and France both exporting/importing cars)
  • IRS + love of variety → gains from trade even between identical countries (pure scale effect)
  • Krugman (1980): monopolistic competition model — firms differentiate products, IRS makes concentration efficient, consumers gain more variety
New trade theory says: even without comparative advantage, trade creates gains through specialization in differentiated goods and exploitation of scale economies.

Labor Force Accounting

Unemployment Rate = Unemployed / (Employed + Unemployed)  |  LFPR = Labor Force / Working-age Pop.
  • Labor force: people aged 16+ who are either employed or actively seeking work — the denominator
  • Not in labor force: students, retirees, discouraged workers, full-time homemakers — invisible in the rate

W2Unemployment Types & Normative Economics
▼

Unemployment rate is never zero — and not all unemployment is bad. Identifying which type you're dealing with determines whether policy can or should respond.

Four Sources of Persistent Unemployment

Frictional

Search and matching take time. Workers and firms need to find each other. Always present in a dynamic economy.

Structural

Skills, geography, or industry mismatch. Coal miners in West Virginia can't instantly become software engineers in Austin.

Wage Floor

Minimum wage or union wage set above market-clearing → excess labor supply at the floor.

Hiring Frictions

Uncertainty, firing costs, and adjustment lags make firms hesitate to hire even when demand exists.

Timing Effects — The Recession Cohort Scar

  • Top-ability graduates: penalty closes by ~10 years — they can switch jobs to catch up
  • Average-ability graduates: slower convergence, lasting wage scar
  • Bottom-ability graduates: higher probability of long-term unemployment and labor-force exit

W3Labor Market: Demand, Supply & Equilibrium
▼

The competitive labor market is the workhorse model. Demand comes from firms' marginal productivity; supply from workers' time-allocation trade-off. Where they meet pins down the wage and employment level.

Labor Demand — Derived from Marginal Productivity

Hire one more worker iff   MR · MPL  ≥  w   ⇒   in competition: P · MPL = w
  • Slopes downward because of diminishing marginal product of labor (MPL): with fixed capital, each additional worker adds less output than the previous one
  • At any wage w, the firm hires the quantity of workers where the value of the marginal worker's output just equals w
  • As w rises, fewer workers clear the bar → demand curve slopes down in (L, w) space
  • Minimum wage evidence: Card & Krueger (1992) found minimal employment effect in NJ fast food; Meer & West (2015) found firms slow hiring rather than fire — adjustment shows up in flows, not the unemployment stock

Labor Supply — The Substitution / Income Decomposition

When the wage rises, two opposing forces act on hours worked. Their relative size determines the slope of the labor supply curve.

Substitution Effect (+)

Leisure becomes more expensive relative to consumption → work more hours. Always positive.

Income Effect (−)

You can afford the same lifestyle with fewer hours → take more leisure. Always negative.

Net Effect

Low/mid wages: substitution wins → curve slopes up. Very high wages: income wins → curve bends backward.

Shape: at high enough wages, an extra ¥ of income is worth less than an extra hour of leisure — the curve bends back toward fewer hours.
  • Empirical relevance: most observed wages sit on the upward-sloping portion. The backward-bending region matters for top earners — surgeons, partners, executives
  • Aggregation: most jobs have fixed contracted hours. Market labor supply reflects mainly the extensive margin (work / don't work) — the participation threshold decision — not continuous hour adjustment on the intensive margin

Equilibrium — Worked Numerical Example

D(w) = 100 − w    S(w) = 2w − 6    →   100 − w = 2w − 6   →   w* = 106/3 ≈ 35.33,   L* = 200/3 ≈ 64.67

Assumption 1: Competitive

Many buyers and sellers — no individual can move the wage. Price-taking behavior on both sides.

Assumption 2: Homogeneous

Workers and firms are interchangeable within the market — no quality differences, no search frictions.

Adjustment

Excess supply (w > w*) → workers undercut → w falls. Excess demand (w < w*) → firms outbid → w rises. Converges to w*.

  • Real-world violations: monopsony (Walmart as the only large employer in a small town can suppress wages below MPL); wage collusion (the famous 2010s Silicon Valley no-poach agreements among Apple, Google, Adobe, Intel)
  • Under monopsony, a binding minimum wage can raise employment — the textbook competitive prediction reverses

Nominal vs. Real Wages — Why "Stagnant" US Wages Are Misleading

Real wage  =  Nominal wage / Price index
  • Nominal wage: the dollar/yen number on your paycheck
  • Real wage: purchasing power of that paycheck against a typical consumption bundle
  • US real wages have been roughly stagnant since the 1970s — a politically charged statistic
  • But total compensation (wages + benefits) grew steadily: health insurance, pension contributions, paid leave
  • US healthcare expenditure ~5× since 1970s (now ~17% of GDP). Employer-paid premiums absorb a large share of what would otherwise be wage gains — so real wages look flat while real compensation rose
Exam takeaway: When asked about "wage stagnation," distinguish nominal vs. real, and wages vs. total compensation. The three measures can move in different directions, and the choice of measure often drives the policy conclusion.

Slides 4Labor Supply: Labor-Leisure Tradeoff Model  NEW Jun 25
▼

The labor-leisure tradeoff is the workhorse model of labor supply at the individual level. It extends the substitution/income framework from W3 into a full utility-maximization setup, and generates the backward-bending supply curve as a prediction, not just an observation.

Model Setup

Maximize U(C, L) s.t. C = w(T − L) + V   [C = consumption, L = leisure hours, T = time endowment, w = wage, V = non-labor income]
  • Time is the scarce resource: every hour not in leisure is an hour of work → C = w · (hours worked) + V
  • Reservation wage: the minimum wage at which a person chooses to work at all. Below it, utility-maximizing choice is L = T (full leisure — don't participate)
  • The reservation wage equals the MRS of consumption for leisure at the endowment point (zero hours of work)

Extensive vs. Intensive Margin

Extensive Margin

Work or not work? Binary participation decision. Responds to reservation wage vs. market wage comparison. Most important for: primary earners, welfare recipients, mothers with young children.

Intensive Margin

How many hours? Conditional on working, how many hours to supply. Responds to wage changes via income and substitution effects. Matters for: overtime, moonlighting, second jobs.

Income vs. Substitution Effects — Decomposed

EffectMechanismDirection on Hours
Substitution EffectWage ↑ → leisure becomes more expensive relative to consumption → substitute work for leisure↑ Hours worked
Income EffectWage ↑ → real income ↑ → afford more of everything including leisure → demand more leisure↓ Hours worked
Net (low wage region)Substitution dominates↑ Upward-sloping supply
Net (high wage region)Income dominates↓ Backward-bending

Policy Distortions to Labor Supply

Welfare Trap

Benefits means-tested against earnings → high effective marginal tax on low earners. Working a few hours reduces benefits by nearly as much as earned → incentive to stay out of the labor force entirely.

EITC (Earned Income Tax Credit)

Phases in with earnings at low incomes (subsidy) → incentivizes entry (↑ extensive margin). Phases out at higher incomes (tax-back) → reduces hours for mid-earners (↓ intensive margin). Net: ↑ participation, ↓ hours among phase-out range.

Historical Experiments

Soviet compulsory employment eliminated the extensive margin entirely. Japan's WWII labor mobilization shows pure substitution effect when coercion removes the income effect — workers supplied maximum hours regardless of wage.

Exam synthesis: The labor-leisure model predicts a backward-bending supply curve at the individual level, but aggregate supply often looks upward-sloping because the extensive margin (participation decisions) dominates at the population level. Higher wages pull more non-participants in, even if those already working don't add hours — the two margins move in opposite directions.

Social choice theory: methods governments use to (1) aggregate people's preferences and (2) make decisions when market failures occur.

Four Allocation Criteria

Cannot improve anyone's satisfaction without worsening someone else's.

Everyone receives the same share.

Equity

No one envies anyone else's allocation.

Satisfies both efficiency and equity simultaneously.

Five Core Axioms

AxiomWhat it requires
ParetoIf everyone prefers A over B, the social choice should too
AnonymitySwapping two people's positions doesn't change the social outcome
NeutralityThe rule doesn't favor any alternative by default
IIARanking of A vs B shouldn't depend on a third option C
MonotonicityIf you rank a candidate higher, they shouldn't lose because of it
W3SCF, SWF & Plurality Rule
▼

Social Choice Function (SCF)

Maps a profile of individual preference orderings to a single social outcome.

Social Welfare Function (SWF)

Maps a profile of preferences to a complete social ranking (not just one winner).

Plurality Rule

Most first-place votes wins. Simple but violates IIA (adding/removing a candidate changes outcome).

  • Condorcet paradox: majority preferences can be cyclical (A>B, B>C, C>A) — no consistent aggregate ranking
  • Every voting rule has weaknesses depending on which axioms it prioritizes
W4Arrow's Impossibility Theorem
▼
Arrow's Theorem (1951): No social welfare function with 3+ alternatives satisfies ALL of: Pareto, Non-dictatorship, IIA, and Unrestricted domain simultaneously.
  • Non-dictatorship: no single individual's preference always determines the social ranking
  • Unrestricted domain: the SWF must work for ALL possible preference profiles (including cyclical ones)
  • Implication: any "reasonable" aggregation rule must violate at least one of these axioms
  • Escape routes: restrict domain (single-peaked preferences), allow cardinal utility (utilitarian SWF), use randomized rules

SEMAdvanced Seminar III: Market Efficiency & Matching Theory  NEW Jun 4
▼

From Advanced Seminar III. Core question: can a market "clear" without being efficient? Answer: yes — because no-loss is a weaker condition than surplus-maximizing matching. Structure, not just rationality, determines outcome quality.

Setup

AgentTypeValue
Buyer 1 (B1)WTP80
Buyer 2 (B2)WTP40
Seller 1 (S1)Cost70
Seller 2 (S2)Cost30

Matching Analysis

Efficient Matching (B1×S2)

B1 buys from S2: surplus = 80−30 = 50. B2 & S1 don't trade (40 < 70). Total surplus = 50.

Inefficient Matching (B1×S1)

B1 buys from S1: surplus = 80−70 = 10. B2 & S2: surplus = 40−30 = 10 (if both transact). Total surplus = 20.

Core Insight: Why Can a Market Settle at an Inefficient Outcome?

  • No-loss ≠ efficient outcome. Even if neither party transacts at a loss, the equilibrium price may fail to route buyers to the lowest-cost sellers
  • If B1 meets S1 first in a decentralized market, B1 may trade at P=75 (no loss for either) — S2 and B2 then may or may not match. Market "cleared" locally but destroyed 30 units of potential surplus
  • This is a search & matching friction problem, not a failure of rationality

Two Key Conclusions

1. Market Structure → Efficiency

Allocation depends on how buyers and sellers are matched, not just whether mutually beneficial trades occur.

Centralized auction → efficient matching. Decentralized bilateral bargaining → path-dependent, friction-prone. Platform design → can force or prevent efficient pairing.

2. Rationality → Distribution, Not Efficiency

Rational agents avoid loss — but don't guarantee finding the best counterparty.

Efficiency = depends on market structure / mechanism. Distribution = depends on rationality + bargaining power.

One-line summary: A market can clear without loss yet still be inefficient — because no-loss is a weaker condition than surplus-maximizing matching. Structure, not just rationality, determines outcome quality.
W3–4Sequential Games, SPNE & Advanced Topics
▼

Subgame Perfect Nash Equilibrium (SPNE)

  • NE that specifies credible strategies in every subgame (rules out empty threats)
  • Found by backward induction: solve from terminal nodes backward to the root
  • Example: entry deterrence — incumbent threatens to fight entry, but the threat is only credible if fighting is a best response after entry

Repeated Games & Folk Theorem

  • In infinitely repeated games, cooperation can be sustained as SPNE if players are patient enough (discount factor δ high)
  • Folk theorem: any feasible, individually-rational payoff can be an equilibrium of an infinitely repeated game
  • Grim trigger strategy: cooperate until defection, then punish forever

Bayesian Games (Incomplete Information)

  • Players have private information (types) drawn from a distribution — others know the distribution but not the realized type
  • Bayesian Nash Equilibrium: strategy profile maximizing expected payoff given beliefs about others' types
  • Applications: auctions, signaling (education as signal of ability), mechanism design
