Japanese Economy

Prof. Hajime Tomura  ·  Visualization: Historical Timeline
Open in Notion
Course Overview — Chronological Timeline
Weekly Drill-Down
W2Post-War Reforms (1945–1949)
▼

GHQ dismantled wartime structures and fought hyperinflation before handing sovereignty back to Japan.

Three GHQ Reforms

Zaibatsu Dissolution

Broke up family-owned conglomerates to reduce monopoly power and democratize economic control.

Land Reform

Redistributed farm land from absentee landlords to tenant farmers → equalized rural income.

Labor Law Reform

Guaranteed workers right to unionize. Seeds of Japan's later lifetime-employment system.

Hyperinflation Mechanism

Wartime: BoJ printed money (RFC bonds)→ Limited supply of goods→ Hyperinflation→ Emergency deposit freeze (1946)→ Inflation continues

Dodge Line (1949)

  • Core idea: balanced budget — no new government bonds, stop RFC lending
  • Cut subsidies on industrial goods → inflation rate fell but caused recession
  • Toyota nearly collapsed. Saved by Korean War procurement (1950–52)

Shoup Report

  • Built progressive income tax system — higher rates for richer people
  • Goal: empower ordinary people, democratize Japan's income distribution
W2–3High Growth Era (1950–1973)
▼

Japan's "economic miracle" — avg real GDP growth ~10%/yr — powered by balanced growth in A, K, and H.

Y = A × F(K, H)    [GDP = Technology × f(Capital, Labor Efficiency)]

↑ H (Labor)

Rural→urban migration, higher education, lifetime employment → skill accumulation via on-the-job training.

↑ K (Capital)

High household savings funneled through banks to industrial investment. Convoy system ensured stability.

↑ A (Technology)

Technology catch-up from the West, absorption of advanced production methods in manufacturing.

Financial System (Convoy System)

  • Financial repression: government capped deposit rates → low lending rates → cheap capital for firms
  • Banks segmented: long-term credit banks (large firms), city banks (working capital), regional banks (SMEs)
  • FILP: postal savings + pension funds → Trust Fund Bureau → Development Bank of Japan → strategic industries
  • Cross-shareholding (Keiretsu): 6 major groups (Mitsubishi, Mitsui, Sumitomo…) — banks own shares in clients for stability

International Capital Controls

  • Bretton Woods: 1$ = 360¥ (fixed rate). Japan needed capital controls to defend the peg.
  • Foreign Exchange Act (1949): firms must deposit foreign currency earnings at government accounts
  • World Bank loans financed bullet trains, highways, dams — circumvented capital controls
W3Collapse of Bretton Woods & Oil Shocks (1971–1979)
▼

The fixed exchange rate system collapsed under US fiscal strain, ending Japan's high-growth era and exposing it to oil price shocks.

Twin Deficit Mechanism (US)

(Y − T − C) − I = (X − M) − (G − T)
  • US fiscal deficit (Vietnam War + social programs) exceeded domestic savings
  • Must be filled by current account deficit → USD accumulated abroad → pressure on gold peg

Nixon Shock → Floating Rates

Aug 1971: US suspends USD–gold convertibility→ Smithsonian Agreement (failed)→ 1973: Major currencies float→ JPY appreciates → exports cool

Japan's Policy Response (IS-LM)

  • BOJ cut policy interest rate → encourage private investment
  • Government expanded expenditure (↑G) → boost aggregate demand
  • Unintended result: land price boom (first post-war bubble, early 1970s)
W4Financial Liberalization & Bubble Formation (1980–1990)
▼

Post-Bretton Woods floating rates made capital controls obsolete. Deregulation redirected bank lending toward real estate — the fuel for the bubble.

Key Causal Chain (exam-critical)

1980: Capital flow liberalization→ 1984: FX futures deregulated→ Bond market opens (domestic+foreign)→ Large firms bypass banks (disintermediation)→ Banks shift to real estate / SME loans→ Over-collateralization on land→ Bubble Economy

Financial Disintermediation

  • Large firms issued bonds directly (cheaper than bank loans) → demand for bank loans collapsed
  • City banks and long-term credit banks lost their core customers
  • Shifted to real estate developers, construction, home buyers, non-bank institutions
  • Inexperienced with new clients → relied on land as collateral → dangerous when land prices fell

Plaza Accord (1985)

  • G5 agreed to depreciate the USD → JPY appreciated sharply (~240→120 ¥/$)
  • BOJ cut rates to counter export slowdown → cheap money flooded into asset markets
W5–6Lost Decade (1990–1998)
▼

Bubble burst in 1990–91, exposing mountains of bad loans backed by collapsed land values. Japan entered a prolonged stagnation.

  • 1989–90: BOJ raised rates to cool bubble → Nikkei collapsed (38,000 → 15,000 by 1992)
  • Bad debt crisis: banks held collateral (land) worth far less than loans outstanding → balance sheet recession
  • Convoy system failure: weak banks propped up by strong ones → delayed resolution, prolonged stagnation
  • 1997 Asian Financial Crisis: triggered second wave of bank failures in Japan; Yamaichi Securities collapsed
  • Fiscal trap: government tried to stimulate (↑G) but structural deflation and balance sheet cleanup dominated
Key lesson: Asset price bubbles built on collateral-based lending create long-lasting balance sheet recessions that monetary policy alone can't fix.

Course Overview — Identification Strategy Flowchart
Weekly Drill-Down
W1Causal Inference Fundamentals
▼

Two Approaches

Model-Based (Structural)

Define how X relates to Y mathematically. Use regression, IV. "I have an idea — can the math prove it?"

Causal Inference

Ask where the variation in X comes from. Tools: RCT, matching, IV, DiD. Focused on identification.

ATE = E[Y(X=1) − Y(X=0)]
  • ITE (Individual Treatment Effect) = generally unknowable → aim for ATE
  • Causality ≠ correlation: correlation is symmetric; causality is directional
  • Data alone can't tell causality — need theory, design, or assumptions
  • Structural estimation: model-based counterfactuals ("what if we build an airport?") — valid only if model is correct
W3Potential Outcomes & Rubin Causal Model
▼
  • Potential outcomes: Y(1) = outcome if treated, Y(0) = outcome if untreated — only one is observable (fundamental problem)
  • Selection bias: naive difference E[Y|X=1] − E[Y|X=0] confounds treatment effect with pre-existing differences
  • Unconfoundedness (ignorability): Y(0),Y(1) ⊥ X | covariates Z → treatment as-good-as-random given Z
  • RCT achieves unconfoundedness by design (random assignment)
W4Endogeneity & Exogeneity
▼
  • Endogeneity: X is correlated with the error term ε → OLS estimate biased
  • Sources: omitted variable bias, reverse causality, measurement error
  • Exogenous variation: variation in X that is "as-good-as-random" for purposes of identifying the causal effect
Core insight: finding exogenous variation in X is the central challenge of all causal inference methods.

International Economics

Dr. Tatyana Chesnokova  ·  Visualization: Trade Theory Mind Map
Open in Notion
Course Overview — Trade Theory Mind Map
Weekly Drill-Down
W1Globalization — Two Waves
▼
Openness = (Imports + Exports) / GDP
WaveDriverKey EventsEnd
19th CenturyTechnologySteamship, Suez Canal (1869), transatlantic cableSmoot-Hawley Tariff (1930)
20th CenturyPolicyGATT/WTO tariff reductions, containerization (1970s)—
Two main theories: (1) Comparative advantage — differences between countries drive trade. (2) Increasing returns to scale — concentration of production in one location is efficient.
W2Comparative Advantage (Ricardian Model)
▼

The most fundamental result in trade theory: trade is driven by relative, not absolute, cost differences.

  • Law of Comparative Advantage: a country should specialize in goods where its opportunity cost is lower
  • One country CANNOT have comparative advantage in both goods (due to reciprocal opportunity costs)
  • Absolute advantage has NO importance for the pattern of trade (but matters for income distribution)
  • Autarky price: equilibrium relative price when trade is banned → found by RS = RD intersection
  • Trade opens: countries face the world relative price → specialize accordingly → both gain
  • "Self-sufficiency" (autarky) arguments like Nigeria's rice import ban are rejected by comparative advantage logic
Pattern of trade determined by comparative advantage, not absolute advantage
W3Relative Supply & Demand in Trade Equilibrium
▼
  • Relative Supply (RS): (Qwine/Qcloth) as a function of (Pwine/Pcloth) — upward sloping
  • Relative Demand (RD): world demand for wine relative to cloth — downward sloping
  • World equilibrium: intersection of RS and RD gives the terms of trade
  • Home exports good with lower autarky relative price, imports the other
Key: the trade equilibrium price lies between the two autarky prices. Both countries gain from trade relative to autarky.
W4Trade & Politics — Winners and Losers
▼
  • Stolper-Samuelson theorem: trade raises the real wage of the abundant factor, lowers that of the scarce factor
  • In rich countries (capital-abundant): trade ↑ returns to capital, ↓ wages of low-skilled labor → political tension
  • Protectionism is a political equilibrium: losing factors lobby for tariffs (e.g., steel workers, farmers)
  • GATT/WTO: multilateral system to credibly commit countries to low tariffs and prevent trade wars
  • Trump tariffs example: 96% of burden passed to US buyers, not foreign exporters
W5Returns to Scale & New Trade Theory
▼
  • Increasing returns to scale (IRS): average costs fall as output rises → tendency toward concentration
  • Intra-industry trade: similar countries trading similar goods (e.g., Germany and France both exporting/importing cars)
  • IRS + love of variety → gains from trade even between identical countries (pure scale effect)
  • Krugman (1980): monopolistic competition model — firms differentiate products, IRS makes concentration efficient, consumers gain more variety
New trade theory says: even without comparative advantage, trade creates gains through specialization in differentiated goods and exploitation of scale economies.

Course Overview — Labor Market Concept Web
Weekly Drill-Down
W1What Is Labor? Definitions
▼
Labor = allocation of time by humans, either explicitly or implicitly in exchange for money, goods, or services. Time is the ultimate scarce resource.

Edge Cases (exam favorites)

CaseLabor?Why
Unpaid internship✓ YesExchange for training/experience (a service)
Cooking for family✓ YesImplicit saving (avoided restaurant cost)
Volunteer workAmbiguousCould be training (yes) or pure leisure (no)
Robot in factory✗ NoNot a human
Sleeping✗ NoNo exchange in marketplace

Labor Force & Unemployment Rate

  • Labor force: over 16, either working or actively looking for work
  • US definition: actively sought work in past 4 weeks
  • Japanese definition: past 1 week, or waiting for results of past activities
  • Unemployment rate can rise for good reasons: wages up → more people enter labor force
W2Unemployment & Normative Economics
▼

Why Unemployment Rate ≠ 0

  • Frictional: search time — workers and firms need time to find each other
  • Structural: skills mismatch between workers and available jobs
  • Minimum wage: government-set wage floor above market-clearing wage → excess supply of labor
  • Incentive issues: risky to hire when future is uncertain

Timing Effects (Key Empirical Finding)

  • Graduating in a recession → earnings penalty persists 10+ years for average graduates
  • Top-ability graduates: catch up after ~10 years
  • Bottom-ability graduates: persistent unemployment; more likely to exit labor force

Positive vs. Normative

PositiveNormative
Observation + structural model; assumes rationality, scarcity, averagenessMoral judgement about how markets should work
"If wage rises, labor demand falls""The minimum wage should be $15"
Falsifiable, objectiveValue-laden, not falsifiable

Efficiency vs. Equality (Boardgame Example)

  • Surplus = WTP − reservation wage = 2000 − 1000 = 1000 yen
  • W1 (pay 1100): efficient ✓, unequal distribution
  • W2 (pay 1500): efficient ✓, more equal distribution
  • W3 (no deal): inefficient ✗ — surplus wasted
  • Efficiency = is the pie maximized? Fairness = how is it split?
W3Labor Market: Demand, Supply & Equilibrium
▼

Labor Demand

  • Slopes down due to diminishing marginal product of labor (fixed capital → each additional worker contributes less)
  • Hire while MR from worker > wage → as wage rises, fewer workers hired
  • Minimum wage debate: Card (1992) found little effect; Meer & West (2015) found firms slow hiring rather than fire

Labor Supply

  • Slopes up: higher wage → more hours / more people enter
  • Backward-bending at very high wages: income effect dominates (earn $100K/hr → prefer leisure)
  • Most jobs have fixed contracted hours → market supply is aggregate of individual threshold decisions

Equilibrium Calculation

D(w) = 100 − w    S(w) = 2w − 6    → w* = 35.33, L* = 64.66 hrs
  • Assumptions: (1) competitive — many buyers and sellers, (2) homogeneous — workers/firms are interchangeable
  • Adjustment mechanism: excess supply → workers undercut → wage falls to w*; excess demand → firms outbid → wage rises
  • Real world violations: monopsonies (Walmart in small towns), wage collusion (tech firms)

Real vs. Nominal Wages

  • Nominal wage: dollar/yen amount. Real wage: purchasing power against a typical bundle
  • US real wages roughly stagnant since 1970s — but total compensation grew via health insurance, pension benefits
  • Healthcare expenditure ~5× since 1970s (now ~17% US GDP) → absorbs wage gains

Public Philosophy & Economic Perspectives (PPEP)

Prof. Takashi Kurihara  ·  Visualization: Social Choice Theory Hierarchy
Open in Notion
Course Overview — Social Choice Theory Hierarchy
Weekly Drill-Down
W1Introduction to Social Choice Theory
▼
Social choice theory: methods governments use to (1) aggregate people's preferences and (2) make decisions when market failures occur.

Four Allocation Criteria

Pareto Efficiency

Cannot improve anyone's satisfaction without worsening someone else's.

Equality

Everyone receives the same share.

Equity

No one envies anyone else's allocation.

Fairness

Satisfies both efficiency and equity simultaneously.

Five Core Axioms

AxiomWhat it requires
ParetoIf everyone prefers A over B, the social choice should too
AnonymitySwapping two people's positions doesn't change the social outcome
NeutralityThe rule doesn't favor any alternative by default
IIARanking of A vs B shouldn't depend on a third option C
MonotonicityIf you rank a candidate higher, they shouldn't lose because of it
W3SCF, SWF & Plurality Rule
▼

Social Choice Function (SCF)

Maps a profile of individual preference orderings to a single social outcome.

Social Welfare Function (SWF)

Maps a profile of preferences to a complete social ranking (not just one winner).

Plurality Rule

Most first-place votes wins. Simple but violates IIA (adding/removing a candidate changes outcome).

  • Condorcet paradox: majority preferences can be cyclical (A>B, B>C, C>A) — no consistent aggregate ranking
  • Every voting rule has weaknesses depending on which axioms it prioritizes
W4Arrow's Impossibility Theorem
▼
Arrow's Theorem (1951): No social welfare function with 3+ alternatives satisfies ALL of: Pareto, Non-dictatorship, IIA, and Unrestricted domain simultaneously.
  • Non-dictatorship: no single individual's preference always determines the social ranking
  • Unrestricted domain: the SWF must work for ALL possible preference profiles (including cyclical ones)
  • Implication: any "reasonable" aggregation rule must violate at least one of these axioms
  • Escape routes: restrict domain (single-peaked preferences), allow cardinal utility (utilitarian SWF), use randomized rules

Game Theory II

Waseda · Advanced strategic analysis  ·  Visualization: Concept Map
Open in Notion
Course Overview — Concept Map
Weekly Drill-Down
W1Review — Game Forms & Nash Equilibrium
▼

Normal Form

Matrix listing players, strategy sets, and payoff functions. Best for simultaneous games.

Extensive Form

Tree with nodes (decision/chance), branches (actions), information sets, and terminal payoffs.

Nash Equilibrium

Strategy profile where no player can gain by unilateral deviation. May not be unique or efficient.

Dominant Strategy

Best response regardless of what others do. If all players have one, dominance is the obvious solution.

W2Classic Games & Strategic Forms
▼

Prisoner's Dilemma

  • Both players have dominant strategy to defect → NE = (Defect, Defect)
  • NE is Pareto-dominated: (Cooperate, Cooperate) is better for both
  • Social implications: arms races, free-riding on public goods, overuse of common resources

Coordination Game

  • Two NE exist: players want to match (e.g., both drive on same side of road)
  • Problem: which equilibrium to coordinate on? → focal points (Schelling), conventions, communication

Key Distinction

Prisoner's dilemma: NE exists but it's inefficient. Coordination game: multiple NE exist — the challenge is selection, not efficiency.
W3–4Sequential Games, SPNE & Advanced Topics
▼

Subgame Perfect Nash Equilibrium (SPNE)

  • NE that specifies credible strategies in every subgame (rules out empty threats)
  • Found by backward induction: solve from terminal nodes backward to the root
  • Example: entry deterrence — incumbent threatens to fight entry, but the threat is only credible if fighting is a best response after entry

Repeated Games & Folk Theorem

  • In infinitely repeated games, cooperation can be sustained as SPNE if players are patient enough (discount factor δ high)
  • Folk theorem: any feasible, individually-rational payoff can be an equilibrium of an infinitely repeated game
  • Grim trigger strategy: cooperate until defection, then punish forever

Bayesian Games (Incomplete Information)

  • Players have private information (types) drawn from a distribution — others know the distribution but not the realized type
  • Bayesian Nash Equilibrium: strategy profile maximizing expected payoff given beliefs about others' types
  • Applications: auctions, signaling (education as signal of ability), mechanism design
